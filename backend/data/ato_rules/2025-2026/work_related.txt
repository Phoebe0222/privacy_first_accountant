Work-Related Expenses — Overview

You can claim a deduction for work-related expenses if you spent the money to earn assessable income, the expense directly relates to earning that income, and you have a record to prove it.

You cannot claim a deduction for:
- Private or domestic expenses (e.g. childcare, personal grooming)
- Expenses relating to earning tax-exempt income
- Capital expenses (generally — though depreciation may apply)

Three-dollar rule: If the total of your work-related expenses that are not required to be substantiated is $300 or less, you can claim a deduction without written evidence. If they exceed $300, you must have written evidence for the entire amount.

The expense must have a direct connection to your income-earning activities. If an expense is partly private and partly work-related, you can only claim the work-related portion.

You cannot claim a deduction for a reimbursed expense — if your employer pays you back, the deduction belongs to the employer, not you.

Common deductible work-related expenses include:
- Home office costs (electricity, internet, decline in value of equipment)
- Vehicle and travel expenses for work purposes
- Clothing (occupation-specific, protective, or compulsory uniforms)
- Self-education directly related to your current job
- Tools, equipment, and technology used for work
- Phone and internet costs used for work
- Subscriptions to professional associations or journals
- Seminars, conferences, and training directly related to your work
