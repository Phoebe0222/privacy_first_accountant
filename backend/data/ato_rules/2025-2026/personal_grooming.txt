Personal Grooming, Health and Fitness

Personal grooming, health and fitness expenses are generally private in nature and are not deductible, even if you feel you need to be well-presented, fit, or healthy to do your job.

Not deductible (in almost all cases):
- Haircuts, hairdressing, cosmetics, makeup, hair and skin care products.
- Gym memberships, personal training, fitness classes, weight-loss programs.
- Private health insurance premiums (a separate tax offset may apply — see the Private Health Insurance offset).
- Prescription glasses, contact lenses, and other general medical or dental expenses.
- Vitamins, supplements, and over-the-counter medication, even if intended to help you cope with work demands.

Limited exceptions (must be directly and specifically required by your job, not just generally beneficial):
- Sunscreen, sunglasses and hats for outdoor workers (e.g. construction, agriculture, outdoor trades) where sun protection is required for the role.
- Fitness or specific medical testing that is a mandatory, ongoing requirement of a physically demanding occupation (e.g. specialist roles in the defence force, police, fire and emergency services) where the employer requires a specific, measurable fitness standard beyond a general expectation of good health.
- Specific protective skin or hygiene products required for hazardous work environments (treated as protective items, not general grooming).

Even where an exception applies, you can only claim the work-related portion, and you must be able to show the expense is required by your specific job — a general policy of "looking professional" or "staying healthy" is not enough.

Records required:
- Evidence the expense is a specific requirement of your role (e.g. employer policy, award, or industry standard).
- Receipts for all claimed items.
