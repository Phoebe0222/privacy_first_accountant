Small Business Income and Deductions

General deductibility principle (ITAA 1997 s8-1):
You can deduct a loss or outgoing to the extent it is incurred in gaining or producing your assessable income, or necessarily incurred in carrying on a business for that purpose.
You cannot deduct capital, private, domestic, or exempt-income expenses.

Common fully deductible business expenses:
- Advertising and marketing (Google Ads, social media, print)
- Bank fees and merchant service fees
- Business travel (accommodation, transport — not private)
- Equipment repairs and maintenance
- Insurance premiums (business-related)
- Legal and accounting fees
- Office supplies and stationery
- Professional subscriptions and memberships
- Salaries, wages, and superannuation paid to employees
- Software subscriptions (SaaS tools used in business)
- Website hosting and domain names

Meals and entertainment (50% rule):
Meal entertainment expenses are generally only 50% deductible under the FBT rules.
Client lunches, team dinners, and staff functions may be limited to 50%.

Superannuation:
Contributions for employees are deductible when paid.
Sole traders and partners can claim personal super contributions if they notify their fund (notice of intent to claim).

Motor vehicle expenses: see vehicle rules.
Home office: see home office rules.

Small business concessions (turnover < $10 million):
- Simplified depreciation: instant asset write-off for eligible assets (threshold confirmed annually).
- Immediate deduction for start-up costs (professional advice, ASIC fees).
- Small business income tax offset for sole traders: up to $1,000 per year.
- Trading stock: simplified rules for low-value stock.

Pre-paid expenses:
If you prepay an expense for a period not exceeding 12 months that ends in the next income year, you can claim the full deduction in the year of payment.

Records required:
Keep records for 5 years from when you lodged the relevant tax return.
Records must explain all transactions and be in English (or translatable).
