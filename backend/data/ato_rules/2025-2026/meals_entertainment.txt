Meals, Entertainment and Functions

As a general rule, the cost of food, drink and entertainment is private in nature and is not deductible — even if you discuss work while eating, or attend a work-related function.

Not deductible (in most cases):
- Daily meals and snacks while at your normal workplace, including overtime meals not covered by an allowance.
- Morning teas, lunches, dinners, drinks or function tickets, even if clients, colleagues or suppliers are present.
- Entertainment-style functions such as Christmas parties, social club events, or sporting/theatre tickets.

When meals can be deductible:
- Overtime meal expenses, where you receive a bona fide overtime meal allowance under an industrial award and the claim does not exceed the ATO's reasonable amount — a record-keeping exception may apply.
- Meals while travelling away from home overnight for work (see travel/accommodation rules) — keep receipts unless covered by a reasonable travel allowance.
- Meals that form part of a deductible self-education activity (e.g. an overnight conference).

Entertainment and FBT (business / sole traders):
- Entertainment expenses (meals, functions, recreation) provided to staff, clients or associates are generally not income-tax deductible, and may attract Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT) if provided to employees.
- Minor, infrequent benefits to employees (e.g. occasional morning tea in the office) may be FBT-exempt, but the income tax deduction is still limited under the entertainment rules.
- The entertainment provisions (Div 32 ITAA 1997) override the general deduction rules — seek specific advice before claiming any meal or entertainment expense as a business deduction.

Records required:
- Receipts showing date, amount, and business purpose.
- For overtime meal claims: evidence of the award allowance received.
