Business Income and Deductions — Overview

General deduction test (s8-1 ITAA 1997): a business expense is deductible if it is incurred in gaining or producing your assessable income, or necessarily incurred in carrying on your business for that purpose — and it is not capital, private/domestic, or related to earning exempt or non-assessable income.

Common deductible business expenses:
- Operating expenses: rent, utilities, wages and salaries, employer super contributions, insurance, repairs and maintenance, advertising and marketing, professional/legal/accounting fees, bank fees, interest on business loans.
- Trading stock (cost of goods sold).
- Motor vehicle expenses for business travel.
- Running costs for a home-based business (apportioned for business use).
- Software and digital product subscriptions used for the business.

Working out taxable income:
Assessable income − allowable deductions = taxable income.
- Assessable income includes ordinary income from business operations (sales, fees, commissions) and statutory income (e.g. assessable balancing adjustments when disposing of depreciating assets).
- Capital expenses (e.g. buying equipment, vehicles, or premises) are generally NOT immediately deductible — they are written off over time under the depreciation/capital allowance rules, though eligible small businesses can use instant asset write-off and the small business depreciation pool.

Other things to get right:
- Prepaid expenses may need to be spread over the period they relate to, depending on the amount and your business size.
- Expenses with both business and private elements must be apportioned — only the business portion is deductible.
- Distinguishing revenue expenses (immediately deductible) from capital expenses (depreciated) is one of the most common areas of error.

Records required: invoices, receipts, and bank statements for all income and expenses, generally kept for 5 years.
