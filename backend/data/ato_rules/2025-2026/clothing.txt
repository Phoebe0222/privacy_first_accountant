Clothing, Laundry and Dry-Cleaning Expenses

You can only claim a deduction for clothing, footwear and accessories that fall into one of these categories:

1. Occupation-specific clothing
Clothing that distinctly identifies you as belonging to a particular profession or trade (e.g. a chef's checked pants, a barrister's robes) — not just clothing you happen to wear at work.

2. Protective clothing and footwear
Items with protective features genuinely necessary to protect you from the risk of illness or injury at work (e.g. hi-vis vests, steel-capped boots, non-slip shoes for nurses, sun-protection clothing for outdoor workers).

3. Compulsory uniforms
A uniform you are required to wear under a workplace policy that is strictly and consistently enforced, and that is distinctive enough to identify you as an employee of that employer (e.g. it carries the employer's logo).

4. Non-compulsory uniforms
Only deductible if the design is registered with AusIndustry on the Register of Approved Occupational Clothing.

Not deductible:
- Conventional clothing — everyday items such as suits, business shirts, jeans, plain trousers, socks and shoes — even if your employer requires you to wear them or you only wear them at work.
- Single items of clothing (e.g. a plain jacket), even with a logo, unless part of a registered or compulsory uniform.

Laundry and dry-cleaning:
- You can claim the cost of washing, drying and ironing eligible work clothing (above), even if your employer supplied the clothing.
- A reasonable estimate (e.g. $1 per load of work-related clothing only, 50 cents per load if mixed with other laundry) can be used without receipts if your total laundry claim is $150 or less, and your total work-related expenses not requiring substantiation are $300 or less.
- Dry-cleaning expenses need receipts regardless of amount.

Records required:
- Receipts for purchase, repair and dry-cleaning of eligible clothing.
- A diary or reasonable basis for laundry estimates if not using receipts.
