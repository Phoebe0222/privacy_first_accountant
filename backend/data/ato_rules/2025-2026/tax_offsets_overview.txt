Tax Offsets — Overview

A tax offset (sometimes called a rebate) directly reduces the amount of tax you have to pay — dollar for dollar. This is different from a deduction, which reduces your taxable income before tax is calculated.

Most tax offsets are non-refundable: they can reduce your tax payable to $0, but any excess is not refunded and (with limited exceptions) cannot be carried forward to a future year.

Most offsets are calculated automatically by the ATO from information already in your tax return or reported by third parties (e.g. Centrelink, your super fund, your health insurer) — you usually don't need to make a separate claim.

Common individual tax offsets include:
- Low income tax offset (LITO) — see below
- Seniors and pensioners tax offset (SAPTO)
- Superannuation-related tax offsets (spouse contributions, super income stream)
- Beneficiary tax offset, and offset for maintaining an invalid or invalid carer
- Private health insurance offset
- Zone and overseas forces tax offsets
- Foreign income tax offset

Low Income Tax Offset (LITO):
- Maximum offset: $700, for taxable income of $37,500 or less.
- Between $37,500 and $45,000: the offset reduces by 5 cents for every $1 over $37,500 (down to $325 at $45,000).
- Between $45,000 and $66,667: the offset reduces by a further 1.5 cents for every $1 over $45,000 (down to $0 at $66,667).
- Applies automatically — no application needed, and it is factored into the ATO's tax calculation and most pay-as-you-go withholding schedules.
